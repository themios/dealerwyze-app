*The pitch sounds simple: an AI answers your phone after hours, captures the lead, and sends you a summary. Here's what it actually looks like in practice - and why the surprise wasn't what we expected.*

---

## 1. What we were trying to solve

Calls come in at 7 p.m. on a Tuesday. You're still on the lot, but you're in the middle of a deal. The phone rings. You can't answer. The caller doesn't leave a voicemail. The lead is gone.

That scenario happens multiple times a week at a small lot. Not because nobody cares. Because one person can't be in two places at once. A missed call at 7 p.m. from a buyer who was ready to talk is a missed deal. There's no polite way to put that.

We weren't looking for a robot to replace us on the phone. We were looking for something that could hold the line when we couldn't - answer the call, be useful, capture the name and number and what they wanted, and hand it off to us in a format we could act on.

---

## 2. The first week

The calls the AI handled were mostly the ones we would have missed anyway. After-hours inquiries about specific cars, people asking if a vehicle was still available, a few callbacks from leads we'd already been texting.

The AI didn't close deals. That was never the point. What it did was make sure we had something to work with in the morning instead of a missed call notification with no name attached.

The summaries it generated were short and useful: caller name (when they gave it), the vehicle they asked about, what they wanted to know, and whether they wanted a callback. That's enough to make the first reply personal instead of generic. "Hi David, you called last night about the 2017 Camry - is a virtual walkthrough this afternoon an option?" lands differently than "Thanks for your interest in our inventory."

---

## 3. What surprised us

Two things we didn't expect.

First: callers weren't bothered by it being an AI. Not a single person complained. Some asked directly "am I talking to a person?" and when the agent said it was an AI assistant for the dealership, the conversation continued normally. People want to be helped. If the AI is helpful, most callers move forward.

Second: the after-hours volume was higher than we thought. We assumed it was a few calls a week. It was more. Buyers shop for cars the same time they do everything else - late evenings, weekends, in gaps between other things. We were dark during exactly those hours. The AI lit that window back up.

---

## 4. Where it doesn't replace a person

For anything that involves negotiation, specific financing questions, or a buyer who has a complicated trade situation - the AI hands it off. It captures the context and moves on. Trying to make an AI close a deal is the wrong use of the technology.

The right use is: every call gets answered, every name and question gets logged, and every callback from the morning list has a context note attached. The dealer calls back knowing what the person wanted instead of calling cold.

That's not AI replacing a salesperson. That's AI making the salesperson's first call more effective.

---

## 5. The net result

After 30 days, the number we looked at was how many leads came through voice that we would have otherwise missed. The answer was enough to justify keeping it running. One turned deal from an after-hours call that the AI captured covers the cost for months.

The subtler benefit is that the lot doesn't go dark when you step away. Buyers who call at 8 p.m. get a response. Buyers who ask if a car is still available get an answer. The first impression isn't a voicemail that says "leave a message and we'll get back to you."

If you're a small lot running on one or two people, the AI voice agent isn't about replacing your team. It's about extending your coverage without extending your hours.
